      Introduction to Thirukural

      Thirukural, also known as the Kural, is a classic Tamil sangam literature consisting of 1,330 couplets or kurals. It was authored by Thiruvalluvar, a poet and philosopher who lived around the 1st century BCE.

      An illustration of ancient Tamil literature

      The work is divided into three main sections:

      • Aram (Virtue): 380 couplets on moral and ethical living
      • Porul (Wealth): 700 couplets on polity, economy, and society
      • Inbam (Love): 250 couplets on love and relationships

      Thirukural is considered one of the greatest works of Tamil literature and has been translated into many languages. It emphasizes universal values and practical wisdom for everyday life.

      Aram (Virtue) - Chapter 1: Invocation

      The first chapter invokes the blessings of God for the composition of the Kural.

      அகர முதல எழுத்தெல்லாம் ஆதி

      அகர முதல எழுத்தெல்லாம் ஆதி
      பகவன் முதற்றே உலகு

      A is the first of all letters; the first of all beings is God; the first of all sciences is the knowledge of letters.

      Kural 1

      கற்றதனால் ஆய பயன் என்கொல்

      கற்றதனால் ஆய பயன் என்கொல்
      வாலறிவன் நற்றாள் தொழாஅர்

      What profit has he who has learned, if he does not worship the good feet of Him who has the sacred knowledge?

      Kural 2

      Aram (Virtue) - Chapter 2: The Glory of Rain

      This chapter praises rain as the source of all life and prosperity.

      தானம் தவம் இரண்டும் ஒருங்கு

      தானம் தவம் இரண்டும் ஒருங்கு
      நின்றால் காணும் அறிவு

      When charity and penance are united, wisdom will be perfected and all virtues will be acquired.

      Kural 3

      மழைக்கு நீர் ஆகி மண்ணுக்கு

      மழைக்கு நீர் ஆகி மண்ணுக்கு
      உரமாகி மன்னுயிர்க்கு ஏமமாகி

      Rain becomes water for the earth, manure for the soil, and food for living beings.

      Kural 4

      Aram (Virtue) - Chapter 3: The Greatness of Ascetics

      This chapter discusses the virtues of ascetics and their way of life.

      இருள்சேர் இருவினையும் சேரா இறைவன்

      இருள்சேர் இருவினையும் சேரா
      இறைவன் பொருள்சேர் புகழ்புரிந்தார்

      The ascetics who have acquired fame by their association with God are free from the darkness of the two kinds of karma.

      Kural 5

      பொருள்சேர் புகழ்புரிந்தார் மாட்டு இறைவன்

      பொருள்சேர் புகழ்புரிந்தார் மாட்டு
      இறைவன் இருள்சேர் இருவினையும்

      Those who have acquired fame through wealth are not free from the darkness of karma, but those who have acquired fame through God are free.

      Kural 6

      Porul (Wealth) - Chapter 39: The Right Scepter

      This chapter discusses the qualities of a good ruler.

      இறைமாட்சி இல்லாள் பொருள் இல்லாள்

      இறைமாட்சி இல்லாள் பொருள்
      இல்லாள் இல்லாள் பொருள் இறைமாட்சி

      Wealth without sovereignty is worthless; sovereignty without wealth is worthless.

      Kural 381

      பொருள் இல்லாள் இறைமாட்சி இல்லாள்

      பொருள் இல்லாள் இறைமாட்சி
      இல்லாள் பொருள் இறைமாட்சி இல்லாள்

      A king without wealth cannot rule; wealth without a king cannot be protected.

      Kural 382

      Inbam (Love) - Chapter 109: The Pre-marital Love

      This chapter describes the beauty and joy of pre-marital love.

      கண்ணின் துஞ்சல் துயிலின் இனிதே

      கண்ணின் துஞ்சல் துயிலின்
      இனிதே கண்ணின் துஞ்சல்

      The sleep of the eyes is sweet; the sleep of love is sweeter.

      Kural 1081

      துஞ்சல் துயிலின் இனிதே கண்ணின்

      துஞ்சல் துயிலின் இனிதே
      கண்ணின் துஞ்சல் துயிலின்

      The sleep that comes from love is sweeter than the sleep of the eyes.

      Kural 1082

      Conclusion

      Thirukural provides timeless wisdom on various aspects of life. Its teachings on virtue, wealth, and love continue to guide people across generations.

      Key Themes

      • Moral and ethical living
      • Importance of education and wisdom
      • Righteous governance
      • Social harmony
      • The beauty of love

      The Kural emphasizes that true wealth comes from virtue and wisdom, not material possessions. It teaches that love is the highest virtue and that a life lived in accordance with dharma leads to ultimate happiness.

      May the wisdom of Thiruvalluvar guide you in your journey through life.

      The Bhagavad Gita - Introduction

      The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse Hindu scripture that is part of the Indian epic Mahabharata. It is a conversation between Prince Arjuna and the god Krishna, who serves as his charioteer.

      Ancient Indian scripture

      The Gita addresses the moral and philosophical dilemmas faced by Arjuna on the battlefield of Kurukshetra. Krishna imparts spiritual wisdom and guidance on duty, righteousness, and the paths to spiritual liberation.

      Key themes include:

      • Dharma (Duty): Righteous action without attachment to results
      • Karma Yoga: The path of selfless action
      • Bhakti Yoga: The path of devotion
      • Jnana Yoga: The path of knowledge

      The Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 2: Sankhya Yoga

      In this chapter, Krishna explains the nature of the soul and the importance of performing one's duty without attachment.

      You have the right to perform your prescribed duties...

      karmaṇy-evādhikāras te mā phaleṣu kadācana

      You have the right to perform your prescribed duties, but you are not entitled to the fruits of your actions.

      Verse 2.47

      Perform your duty without attachment to the results...

      yogasthaḥ kuru karmāṇi saṅgaṃ tyaktvā dhanañjaya

      Perform your duty without attachment to the results, for by working without attachment one attains the Supreme.

      Verse 2.48

      Krishna teaches Arjuna that true wisdom lies in understanding the impermanence of the material world and the eternal nature of the soul.

      The Bhagavad Gita - Chapter 6: Dhyana Yoga

      This chapter discusses meditation and self-control as means to achieve spiritual realization.

      For the yogi who has conquered the mind...

      yadā viniyataṃ cittam ātmany-evāvatiṣṭhate

      When the mind is controlled and fixed on the Self alone, the yogi is said to be in yoga.

      Verse 6.18

      The yogi whose mind is fixed on Me...

      yogī yuñjīta satatam ātmānaṃ rahasi sthitah

      The yogi should constantly engage his mind in meditation, remaining alone in a secluded place.

      Verse 6.10

      The chapter emphasizes the importance of steady practice and detachment in the path of meditation.

      The Bhagavad Gita - Conclusion

      The Bhagavad Gita concludes with Krishna revealing his divine form to Arjuna and emphasizing the importance of devotion and surrender to God.

      Key Lessons

      • Fulfill your duties selflessly
      • Control your mind and senses
      • Surrender to the divine will
      • Seek knowledge of the Self

      The Gita teaches that true peace comes from understanding one's true nature and living in harmony with dharma. It remains a timeless guide for spiritual seekers across the world.

      May the teachings of the Bhagavad Gita illuminate your path to wisdom and liberation.

      The Ramayana - Introduction

      The Ramayana is an ancient Indian epic poem composed by the sage Valmiki. It narrates the life of Rama, an avatar of the god Vishnu, and his journey to rescue his wife Sita from the demon king Ravana.

      The great Indian epic

      The story is divided into several kandas (books) and explores themes of duty, honor, love, and the triumph of good over evil.

      Main characters:

      • Rama: The protagonist, embodiment of dharma
      • Sita: Rama's devoted wife
      • Lakshmana: Rama's loyal brother
      • Hanuman: The devoted monkey god
      • Ravana: The antagonist, king of Lanka

      The Ramayana - The Exile

      Rama is exiled to the forest for fourteen years due to a promise made by his father, King Dasharatha. Sita and Lakshmana accompany him in his exile.

      Rama's resolve in exile

      nāhaṃ kāryaṃ tyajāmi kadācana

      I never abandon my duty, no matter what the circumstances.

      During their stay in the forest, Sita is abducted by Ravana, leading to the great battle that forms the climax of the epic.

      The Ramayana teaches the importance of keeping promises, protecting the innocent, and maintaining righteousness even in adversity.

      The Ramayana - The Battle and Victory

      Rama, with the help of an army of monkeys led by Hanuman, wages war against Ravana to rescue Sita.

      Hanuman's devotion

      jaya śrī rāma

      Victory to Lord Rama

      After defeating Ravana, Rama returns to Ayodhya with Sita and is crowned king. The Ramayana ends with the establishment of Rama's rule, symbolizing the victory of dharma.

      The epic emphasizes virtues like loyalty, courage, and devotion, and continues to inspire millions with its moral teachings.
